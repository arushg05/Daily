Financial market analysis relies heavily on the empirical study of price action, wherein the 
collective psychology of market participants is permanently recorded in the form of geometric 
chart structures and candlestick formations.1 Over decades of market observation, beginning 
with the foundational theories of Charles Dow and Japanese rice merchants, technical analysts 
have codified these historical footprints into a vast taxonomy of patterns that forecast trend 
continuations, bilateral indeterminacies, and macroeconomic reversals.1 However, isolated 
pattern recognition is inherently susceptible to false signals, market noise, and institutional 
manipulation. To achieve a statistical edge and robust risk management, these structural 
patterns must be systematically mapped against empirical technical indicators—specifically 
those measuring momentum, volume, volatility, and trend strength.5 
The premise of this analysis is to provide an exhaustive, encyclopedic detailing of all identified 
candlestick patterns, classical chart geometries, exotic formations, advanced Fibonacci-based 
harmonic structures, and complex market cycle frameworks (such as Wyckoff Theory). 
Furthermore, it defines the precise technical conditions, indicator confluences, and execution 
rules required to trade them systematically, neutralizing emotional bias through strict algorithmic 
parameters. 
The Epistemology of Candlestick Formations and 
Indicator Confluence 
Candlesticks serve as the atomic units of price action, capturing the open, high, low, and close 
of an asset within a defined temporal period.8 The relationship between the real body (the 
open-to-close range) and the shadows or wicks (the intraday extremes) reveals the immediate 
balance of power between buyers and sellers.6 Candlestick patterns are broadly categorized 
into single, dual, and multi-candle formations, each carrying bullish, bearish, or 
neutral/indecision biases. 
Trading raw candlestick patterns in isolation is statistically suboptimal and exposes capital to 
unnecessary drawdown.11 Rigorous execution methodologies demand indicator confluence to 
filter false signals and measure the underlying market physics. For instance, moving averages 

================================================================================
establish the baseline trend context, momentum oscillators like the Relative Strength Index 
(RSI) or Stochastic Oscillator quantify overbought or oversold exhaustion, and volume indicators 
such as On-Balance Volume (OBV) or the MACD histogram validate institutional participation.12 
When a candlestick pattern aligns perfectly with its complementary technical indicator, the 
probability of a successful trade increases exponentially.5

Macro Market Geometry: Classical Chart Patterns 
While candlestick patterns define localized, short-term psychological dynamics, classical chart 
patterns illustrate intermediate-to-macro supply and demand transitions.1 These patterns require 
multiple weeks or months to form and resolve, mapping the trajectory of massive institutional 
accumulation and distribution phases.1 Classical patterns are divided into three core categories: 
Reversal, Continuation, and Bilateral. 
Trading classical chart patterns relies heavily on algorithmic breakout rules to filter out market 
noise and avoid "bull traps" or "bear traps" (false breakouts). The most critical validation metric 
is the Volume Surge Rule: a legitimate breakout from any classical boundary must be 
accompanied by a volume expansion of at least 50% above the 20-day moving average.18 
Furthermore, the 3% Penetration Rule demands that price must close decisively beyond the 
breakout boundary by a margin of at least 3% to confirm immediate acceptance of the new price 
level.27 
Reversal Chart Geometries 
Reversal patterns indicate that the prevailing macroeconomic trend has reached structural 
exhaustion. The underlying psychology shifts from aggression to distribution (at market tops) or 
from capitulation to accumulation (at market bottoms).19

interconnected price swings (labeled X, A, B, C, D).56 
Unlike classical patterns which rely on reactive breakouts, harmonic patterns are highly 
predictive reversal structures. They define a mathematically exact Potential Reversal Zone 
(PRZ) at point D, allowing traders to enter positions precisely as the trend exhausts itself at a 
predefined liquidity node.58 To be classified as a valid harmonic structure, the individual legs 
must adhere to strict geometric tolerances; deviations render the pattern invalid.58 
The Harmonic Taxonomy and Execution Protocols 
Harmonic trading is heavily systematic. The execution protocol at the PRZ dictates that traders

Wolfe Waves: The Physics of Price Equilibrium 
Identified by Bill Wolfe, the Wolfe Wave is an esoteric 5-wave structural pattern based on 
Newton's first law of physics, demonstrating an equilibrium-seeking mechanism in price action.70 
The pattern requires an equidistant price channel.72 Waves 1, 2, 3, and 4 define the primary 
structural channel. The defining characteristic is Wave 5, which must intentionally breach the 
channel boundaries in a false breakout (a classic institutional stop-hunting maneuver) before 
violently reverting.71 
●​ Optimal Indicator Confluence: MACD and RSI. A valid Wolfe Wave must show 
divergence on the MACD histogram and RSI during the formation of Wave 5, proving the 
breakout is false and lacks momentum.71 
●​ Execution Conditions: Entry occurs immediately as Wave 5 reverses and re-enters the 
primary channel established by Waves 1-3.71 Stop-loss is placed just beyond the extreme 
tip of Wave 5. The defining feature of the Wolfe Wave is its highly precise profit target, the 
Estimated Price at Arrival (EPA). This is found by drawing a straight trendline connecting 
the apex of Point 1 and Point 4 and extending it into the future.33 
Wyckoff Theory: The Anatomy of Market Cycles 
Richard Wyckoff's methodology transitions technical analysis from mere geometric pattern 
matching into deep market micro-structure analysis.73 Wyckoff posited that markets are heavily 
manipulated by the "Composite Man" (representing institutional capital) through repetitive, 
engineered cycles of Accumulation, Markup, Distribution, and Markdown.74 To navigate these 
cycles, Wyckoff delineated patterns into distinct Phases (A through E) marked by specific, 
observable price and volume events.75 
The Wyckoff Accumulation Schematic (Market Bottoms): 
●​ Phase A (Stopping the Downtrend): Initiated by Preliminary Support (PS) as institutions 
begin buying, culminating in a violent Selling Climax (SC) where retail capitulation is fully 
absorbed on massive volume.75 This is followed by an Automatic Rally (AR) and a 
Secondary Test (ST) of the SC lows on diminished volume.77 
●​ Phase B (Building a Cause): A prolonged, choppy consolidation phase where institutions 
systematically accumulate assets without driving the price up, establishing a trading 
range.73 
●​ Phase C (The Test/Shakeout): The defining moment of the schematic, featuring the 
Spring. The Spring is a deliberate, momentary plunge below the Phase B support floor 
designed to trigger retail stop-losses and trap breakout traders.75 If the Spring recovers 
quickly on low volume, the institutional accumulation is complete.75 
●​ Phase D (Trend Transition): Price breaks upward, demonstrating a Sign of Strength 
(SOS) on massive volume.75 Pullbacks to new support floors are marked as the Last Point 
of Support (LPS).75 

================================================================================
●​ Phase E (Markup): The asset definitively breaks out of the accumulation range, initiating 
the macroeconomic uptrend.73 
The Wyckoff Distribution Schematic (Market Tops): Operating as the inverse of 
accumulation, institutions offload inventory to late-arriving retail participants.73 It is characterized 
by Preliminary Supply (PSY) and a massive Buying Climax (BC) driven by retail euphoria.75 
Instead of a Spring, Phase C features the Upthrust After Distribution (UTAD)—a fake 
breakout above resistance to trap late buyers before the fatal markdown begins.75 This is 
followed by the Sign of Weakness (SOW) and the Last Point of Supply (LPSY), confirming 
the transition to a bear market.75 
Indicator Alignment for Wyckoff Execution: Wyckoff theory relies almost exclusively on Price 
Action and Volume (OBV/MFI). Volume must spike during climactic events (SC, BC, Spring, 
UTAD) and contract completely during tests (ST) and consolidations, revealing the footprint of 
the Composite Man.74 
The Architecture of Indicator Confluence and System 
Design 
The true efficacy of pattern trading lies in the absolute rejection of indicator redundancy and the 
embrace of orthogonal technical measurements.21 Utilizing multiple indicators from the same 
category (e.g., mapping RSI, MACD, and Stochastics simultaneously on a single chart) creates 
a dangerous illusion of confirmation known as indicator redundancy, where all tools simply 
reflect the same underlying momentum math.21 
A robust algorithmic execution system must synthesize exactly one tool from each distinct 
mathematical category to validate a chart or candlestick pattern.7 
1.​ Trend Filters (Exponential Moving Averages - EMA/SMA): No continuation pattern (e.g., 
Bull Flag, Ascending Triangle) should be taken against the primary trend. The 50-day and 
200-day SMAs serve as baseline directional filters. A pattern occurring above the 200-SMA 
has a significantly higher probabilistic win rate for long trades.7 
2.​ Momentum Filters (RSI or MACD): Reversal patterns (e.g., Double Bottoms, Falling 
Wedges, Engulfing Candles) must feature bullish divergence on the RSI or the MACD 
histogram, proving that downward momentum is mathematically exhausting before the 
price physically reverses.5 
3.​ Volume Confirmation (OBV or Raw Volume): Volume is the ultimate arbiter of 
institutional intent. Breakouts from chart patterns must register at least 150% of the 20-day 
average volume to be validated.18 Alternatively, the On-Balance Volume (OBV) indicator 
can confirm if cumulative capital flows support the geometric price trajectory.6 
4.​ Volatility Assessment (ATR & Bollinger Bands): The Average True Range (ATR) 
dictates the objective placement of stop-losses.7 By placing stops 1.5x or 2x the ATR 

================================================================================
beyond a pattern's structural invalidation point (e.g., below the Head in a Head & 
Shoulders), traders immunize their capital against normal market noise and algorithmic 
stop-hunting wicks.7 Bollinger Bands compress heavily during bilateral patterns, and a 
subsequent band expansion visually confirms the breakout velocity.12 
By rigidly enforcing these exact confluence parameters—pairing specific geometries with their 
mathematical counterparts—analysts transform subjective chart art into an objective, statistically 
robust trading architecture.
